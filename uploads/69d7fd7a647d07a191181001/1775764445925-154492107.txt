Q1(a). [3]

Ans 1(a):

i. Identify and define the OB model MotoCorp is following:
MotoCorp is following the Custodial Model of Organizational Behaviour.
This model is based on the economic resources of the organization, where employees' needs for security and welfare are fulfilled. Managers focus on providing job security and benefits, which results in employees' dependence on the organization rather than on managerial authority alone.

ii. Advantages and Disadvantages of this model:
Advantages:
Provides job security and stable employment.
Creates a sense of loyalty and dependence on the organization.
Reduces resistance to authority, as employees feel secure.

Disadvantages:
Employees may feel passive and dependent, leading to low motivation and creativity.
Lack of challenge and empowerment can reduce innovation and initiative.
Productivity may remain at a mediocre level.

iii. Suggest a shift to a more suitable OB model:
MotoCorp should shift to the Supportive Model.
This model emphasizes leadership, motivation, and participation, rather than just authority or security.
It encourages employee involvement, creativity, and job satisfaction.
By empowering employees and valuing their contributions, the organization can move from passive cooperation to active engagement and higher productivity.

Ans 1(b):
Definition: Organizational Behaviour is a field of study that investigates the impact that individuals, groups, and structure have on behaviour within organizations, for the purpose of applying such knowledge toward improving an organization's effectiveness.

Scope:

Individual Behaviour: Focuses on personality, perception, learning, values, attitudes, and motivation of employees.
Example: Understanding why one employee is highly motivated while another shows apathy.

Group Behaviour: Studies group dynamics, leadership, teamwork, communication, conflict, and decision-making.
Example: How team spirit and effective communication improve performance in a project team.

Organizational System: Examines the structure, culture, power, politics, and overall processes of the organization.
Example: A flat organizational structure encouraging innovation versus a hierarchical structure emphasizing discipline.

Organizational Change and Development: Deals with managing change, stress, innovation, and organizational development programs.
Example: Training employees to adapt to digital transformation initiatives.

Ans 2(a):
Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (Pyramid from bottom to top):

Level 1 - Physiological Needs (Base): Air, Water, Food, Shelter, Clothing, Sleep, Reproduction. These are the fundamental biological requirements for survival.

Level 2 - Safety Needs: Personal Security, Health and Wellbeing, Stable Environment, Financial Security, Safety Nets. Relates to security and predictability in one's environment.

Level 3 - Love and Belonging Needs: Family, Friendship, Community, Romantic Relationships, Group Membership, Affection and Intimacy. Relates to social connection and acceptance.

Level 4 - Esteem Needs: Self Respect, Confidence, Respect from Others, Reputation, Recognition and Achievement. Relates to recognition, competence, and respect.

Level 5 - Self-Actualization (Top): Creativity, Personal Growth, Moral Development, Pursuit of Meaning, Purpose Driven Action, Peak Experience (joy, transcendence, insight). Relates to realising one's full potential.

Ans 2(b):
Attitude is a learned predisposition to respond positively or negatively toward a person, object, or situation. It has three components:

Cognitive Component: Refers to the beliefs, thoughts, or knowledge a person has about something.
Example: "Working in a team increases productivity."

Affective Component: Refers to the emotions or feelings a person has toward something.
Example: Feeling happy and motivated when assigned a challenging project.

Behavioural Component: Refers to the intent to act or observable actions based on beliefs and feelings.
Example: Actively participating in team discussions or offering help to colleagues.


Q2(c). [3]

Ans 2(c):

i. If you apply only Theory X to the whole team:
Theory X assumes employees are lazy, need constant supervision, and avoid responsibility. Applying it to the entire team would demotivate the self-motivated, creative employees, stifle initiative, and reduce overall productivity.

ii. If you apply only Theory Y, what risks might occur:
Theory Y assumes employees are self-motivated, responsible, and enjoy work. Applying it to the dependent employees who require guidance may lead to confusion, mistakes, and lack of discipline, as they may struggle without clear supervision.

iii. How to balance leadership style between Theory X and Y:
Use an adaptive approach:
Apply Theory X management (close supervision, clear instructions) to employees who need guidance.
Apply Theory Y management (delegation, empowerment, encouragement of creativity) to self-motivated employees.
This approach maximizes productivity while maintaining motivation and creativity across the team.

Four organizational practices that can help employees with low self-esteem gain confidence:

Training and Skill Development: Provide workshops, on-the-job training, and mentorship programs to help employees gain competence and feel capable.

Positive Feedback and Recognition: Regularly acknowledge achievements, effort, and improvement to reinforce self-worth.

Assigning Challenging but Achievable Tasks: Gradually increasing responsibility helps employees experience success and build confidence.

Encouraging Participation in Decision-Making: Involve employees in team discussions and decisions to make them feel valued and capable.

Personality Profile of an Ideal IT Project Manager

1. Big Five Personality Traits:

Openness to Experience: High
Willing to explore innovative solutions, adapt to new technologies, and embrace creative problem-solving.

Conscientiousness: Very High
Organized, dependable, detail-oriented, and capable of delivering projects on time.

Extraversion: Moderate to High
Effective in communication, motivating team members, and stakeholder management without being overly dominant.

Agreeableness: High
Cooperative, supportive, and able to maintain positive team relationships while resolving conflicts diplomatically.

Neuroticism (Emotional Stability): Low
Calm under pressure, resilient to stress, and able to make rational decisions in challenging situations.

2. Locus of Control:
Internal Locus of Control: High
Believes outcomes depend on their own actions, takes responsibility for project success, and proactively addresses challenges.

3. Key Dimensions of Emotional Intelligence (EI):

Self-Awareness: Recognizes own strengths, weaknesses, and emotions to manage behavior effectively.
Self-Regulation: Maintains composure, handles stress, and responds thoughtfully rather than reactively.
Motivation: Intrinsically driven to achieve goals, inspire the team, and pursue continuous improvement.
Empathy: Understands team members' perspectives and needs, facilitating better communication and conflict resolution.
Social Skills: Builds strong relationships, influences stakeholders, negotiates effectively, and fosters teamwork.

Summary:
An ideal IT Project Manager is organized, adaptable, and responsible, maintains emotional stability under pressure, communicates effectively, motivates the team, and takes proactive control of project outcomes while showing empathy and collaborative leadership.
